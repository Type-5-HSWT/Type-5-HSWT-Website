About HSWT

The Irish power system targets 80% renewables by 2030, based mainly on onshore/offshore wind and solar PV generation. High levels of converter-based generation lead to power system stability concerns, but EirGrid, transmission system operator, targets increasing the system non-synchronous penetration (SNSP) limit from 75% to 95%. At present, synchronous condensers are the preferred option to support high SNSP ambitions, as a well-established technology. However, cost considerations encourage more innovative technologies, such as grid-forming converters, but a clear grid-forming definition is lacking, and equipment cost vs. capability considerations remains active research. Instead, here, Type 5 wind turbines are investigated as a robust and economic solution towards achieving 100% renewables ambitions. Type 5 turbines replace the converter and gearbox of Type 3 and 4 wind turbines with a mechanical "hydraulic fluid" transmission, which connects the variable speed aerodynamic rotor to a directly grid-connected synchronous machine, such that all the benefits of synchronous machines apply, e.g. high fault current, inertial response. High-fidelity electro-mechanical models of Type 5 wind turbines, and their control systems, will be developed, and their ability to meet grid code (and system service) requirements will be evaluated, in comparison with Type 3 and 4 (converter-based) wind turbines. The implications of large Type 5 deployments on fast frequency reserve requirements, and other system services, as well as achieving very high SNSP levels will be assessed. Finally, techno-economic analysis will determine the optimal combination of Type 5 turbines, for reducing reliance on grid-forming converters and synchronous condensers for the future Irish power system.
